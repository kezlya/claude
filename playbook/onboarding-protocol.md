# Onboarding Protocol

## Entry Questions (Skool)

**Q1:** "Tell me about the automation or AI system you're running right now. What does it do, and what's been the most frustrating thing about it lately?"

**Q2:** "When something goes wrong with your setup, what do you usually do first?"

**Q3:** "How many people or clients are depending on your automation right now, and what is your ideal target or goal?"

## Framing (goes on the About page, NOT on the question form)

Skool has no framing space on the question form itself. The About page must set the context before applicants reach the questions. The About page should make clear:

- This community is for founders who have built something real with AI automation
- They are hitting the ceiling that tutorials and no-code tools don't address
- This is where Google/Microsoft-scale engineering thinking meets the no-code world
- The community is selective - not everyone gets in

The About page creates the frame. The questions qualify within that frame.

## How to Read Answers

### Q1 - Production reality and pain

| Answer quality | What it means | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Specific system, specific frustration, names tools | Real operator with real pain - ICP | Approve |
| Vague ("I use some automations") | Likely a browser or beginner | Phase 1: maybe. Phase 2: reject |
| Very detailed engineering language | Too technical - probably doesn't need us | Consider carefully |
| "I'm interested in learning" / "Nothing yet" | Not ICP | Don't approve |

### Q2 - Technical level (through behavior)

| Answer pattern | Technical level | ICP fit |
|---|---|---|
| "I check the execution logs, try to figure out which step failed, Google the error" | Semi-technical (sweet spot) | Yes |
| "I SSH into the server, check container logs, debug the code" | Too technical | Probably not |
| "I contact my developer" / "I wait and restart" | Too non-technical | Probably not |
| "I check forums, ask in communities, try different approaches" | Semi-technical, resourceful | Yes |

### Q3 - Scale, stakes, and ambition

| Answer pattern | What it reveals | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| "5-10 clients, want to grow to 50" | Real stakes, growth ambition, potential multiplier | Strong |
| "Our team of 15, plus about 200 customers" | Real business, high cost-of-failure | Very strong (retainer candidate) |
| "Just me, want to get my first clients" | Pre-revenue, low stakes | Phase 1: maybe. Phase 2: reject |
| "3 enterprise clients, targeting 10" | High-value clients, scaling pressure | Very strong (retainer candidate) |

## Approval Mindset

The free community is NOT a waiting room for paid. The filter is **"do they have real stakes?"** - not "will they pay?"

Someone who never upgrades to paid but has 10 clients with real automation pain is valuable because they generate:
- **Content** - their real problems become posts, case studies, and proof
- **Word of mouth** - their clients and network hear about Vitaly
- **Reputation** - solving their problem publicly demonstrates depth

Don't filter for budget. Filter for real problems with real consequences.

## Approval Protocol

1. **Check pending applications daily** (part of morning standup)
2. **Respond within 24 hours** - speed matters more than perfection
3. **Send a personal diagnostic note on every approval:**
   - Reference their Q1 answer specifically
   - One sentence showing you understand their problem
   - Example: "You mentioned your Make scenarios crash at 100+ orders - that's usually a concurrency problem, not a workflow problem. Welcome - post about it and let's dig in."
   - This is the first proof of the product. It separates this community from every other Skool group.
4. **Flag hot signals immediately** - if Q1 describes retainer-level pain ($10K+ at stake, enterprise pressure, scaling emergency), note it for pipeline follow-up
5. **Rejection is silence** - don't approve people who don't match, no rejection message needed

## Phase Transitions

**Phase 1 (first 10 approved members):**
- Be slightly generous with approvals
- Need discussion energy and pattern data
- Accept people whose answers are mediocre but not red flags
- Use this phase to learn what good vs bad answers actually look like

**Phase 2 (after 10+ members with pattern data):**
- Tighten the filter
- Only approve people whose Q1 describes a real, specific problem
- Q3 should show real dependents with growth ambition
- Signal-based trigger, not calendar-based
