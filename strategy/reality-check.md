# Reality Check - Original vs New Strategy

**Date:** March 9, 2026
**Purpose:** Honest comparison of the two strategies

---

## Original Strategy (spec-v1, January 2026)

### What it proposed
- Community called "Agent Orchestrator" on Skool
- Free tier (curated) + paid VIP at $500/month
- Paid tier launched "when 5 people ask"
- Backend: Remote Dev Team agency ($10K+ engagements)
- Revenue target: $10K/month by December 2026
- Moderators: Remote Dev Team employees (compensated as part of their job)
- Two audiences: frustrated technical builders + non-technical entrepreneurs

### Reality check - what was wishful thinking

1. **$500/month with no proven audience.** $500/month is a hard sell for a brand-new community with no track record. You need massive trust before someone pays $6K/year to join a Skool group. Most successful $500+ communities launched after the founder already had a large audience (10K+ followers, YouTube channel, etc.). You didn't have that distribution yet.

2. **"Launch paid tier when 5 people ask."** This is a procrastination trigger disguised as strategy. It creates no urgency, no timeline, no forcing function. You could run free forever waiting for 5 people to explicitly say "I want to pay $500/month." People rarely volunteer to pay - you have to put a price in front of them.

3. **Agency backend as primary monetization.** Remote Dev Team agency projects ($10K+) require a sales pipeline, project management, contractor coordination, and delivery infrastructure. The community-to-agency funnel assumes members convert into clients who want full dev teams built. That's a long sales cycle with high friction. The community doesn't naturally produce these leads - it produces people who want advice, not outsourced development.

4. **Two audiences in one community is risky.** "Frustrated technical builders" and "non-technical entrepreneurs" have fundamentally different vocabularies, expectations, and needs. Serving both dilutes the value prop for each. Most successful niche communities pick one and go deep.

5. **Revenue target of $10K/month by December 2026 was vague.** No clear breakdown of where the $10K comes from. "Agency leads" is hand-wavy. No month-by-month model. No realistic ramp. $10K/month from a new community + agency in 9 months is possible but the spec had no plan to get there.

6. **Moderator plan was an afterthought.** "Part of their job initially, bonus/commission when profitable" - no structure, no agreement, no incentives. This doesn't attract motivated moderators. It attracts employees doing the minimum because their boss asked.

7. **No ad budget or paid growth plan.** The growth strategy was LinkedIn posts + word of mouth + university talks. Organic-only growth for a niche community is slow. Without paid distribution, hitting 100 members by June 2026 was optimistic.

8. **Content strategy depended entirely on Vitaly.** Weekly hot seats, live builds, community posts - all from one person. No plan for what happens when the founder burns out or gets busy with consulting work. The n8n content repurposing pipeline was marked "aspirational" - meaning it wasn't going to happen at launch.

### What was good about the original
- Correct identification of the niche (AI agents/automation for builders)
- Vitaly's credentials are genuinely strong and differentiated
- The founder-led, practitioner-not-creator positioning is solid
- Application-based onboarding filters for quality
- Core principles (ship over perfection, no politics/drama) are clear

## New Strategy (school-strategy-brief, March 2026)

### What it proposes
- Platform: school.com (or alternatives - still deciding)
- Free community (organic growth funnel) + paid at $250/month, 20-member hard cap
- 3 moderators (Ed as lead + 2 others), 70/30 MRR split, 3-phase program
- Primary revenue: Fractional CTO retainers ($4K-$10K/month per package)
- Digital products: courses ($27-$397), template packs ($67)
- Free community marketplace (mods sell services, 10% platform cut)
- Ad budget: $800/month
- Full month-by-month income model

### Reality check - what's strong

1. **The CTO funnel math works.** Community at 20 members x $250 = $5K gross. CTO packages at 3-5 clients = $15K-$32K/month. The community costs $800/month in ads and generates CTO clients for free. This is a real business model, not a "hope people pay" model. The community is the marketing, not the product.

2. **$250/month is the right price point.** High enough to filter for serious people. Low enough that technical founders/CTOs don't blink. At $500, you're competing with executive coaching. At $99, you're competing with every AI newsletter. $250 is the sweet spot for "premium community, not premium coaching."

3. **20-member cap is honest and smart.** Instead of pretending you can serve 100+ people personally, you acknowledged the cognitive limit. This creates scarcity (waitlist model), justifies the premium price, and ensures every member gets real attention. The cap also makes the CTO conversion rate realistic - you actually know these 20 people.

4. **Moderator program is fully designed.** 3-phase plan, clear bonuses, activity minimums, content ownership buckets, non-poaching rules. Ed knows exactly what he's getting into. The Phase 1 no-cash period is honest - you're not promising money you don't have. The milestone bonuses are small but motivating.

5. **Month-by-month income model exists.** You can actually track against projections. The spreadsheet shows when community breaks even (Month 4), when mods hit $500/month (Month 6), when CTO revenue kicks in (Month 3-4). This is a plan, not a wish.

6. **Fractional CTO is what you actually do.** The original strategy routed monetization through agency projects (Remote Dev Team), which requires project management, contractors, and delivery. CTO retainers are just you giving advice and leadership - something you already do. No additional infrastructure needed. Zero delivery risk.

7. **Digital products as passive income layer.** Courses and templates are build-once, sell-forever. They don't scale the community beyond 20 members but they add $1K-$4K/month once built. Smart secondary revenue.

8. **Rejected strategies section shows thinking depth.** You didn't just pick $250 - you modeled $99, $149, $199, and $500 and documented why each was wrong. You considered and rejected large community, mod retainers, individual performance tracking, and other approaches. This is a mature strategy.

### Reality check - what to watch out for

1. **CTO client acquisition timeline may be optimistic.** The model shows first CTO client at Month 3-4 from the community funnel. In reality, trust-to-hire cycle for $4K-$10K/month retainers can take 3-6 months of relationship building. The community members who join in Month 1-2 might not convert until Month 6-8. Plan for a slower ramp on CTO revenue.

2. **Mod income ceiling is real.** At $500-516/month, mods are doing real work (5 posts/week, 3 out of 4 calls, 48hr response to intros) for part-time-job-level pay. Ed needs to understand this is a reputation-building play, not an income play. If mods expect $1K+, they'll be disappointed. The strategy brief acknowledges this but it bears repeating in the agreement.

3. **Platform decision is still open.** "School.com vs Skool vs Circle vs custom" is a meaningful choice. Each has different pricing, features, and limitations. This needs to be decided before you can build anything. Don't let this become analysis paralysis.

4. **$800/month ad spend with no proven funnel.** You're budgeting $800/month for ads before you know which channel works (LinkedIn, Meta, Google). Early months should test small ($200-300) across channels before committing $800 to one. The model assumes ads work from Month 1 - they might not.

5. **Digital products are "estimated $1K-4K/month once built" but building is work.** A $397 course on "AI-Powered Deep Research for Entrepreneurs" requires recording, editing, hosting, and marketing. Template packs require documentation and support. These aren't free to create. Plan the build sequence carefully.

6. **Free community growth of 67/month in Months 1-3 requires distribution.** Where are 67 new free members per month coming from? LinkedIn alone? Ads? Referrals? The growth model needs a channel attribution plan.

7. **Year 2 projection of 6 CTO packages and $44K/month is aggressive.** Going from 4 to 6 CTO packages means ~48 billable hours/month. That's nearly full-time client work plus running a community. Quality and availability will be stretched.

---

## Verdict

The new strategy is significantly more realistic. The core insight is correct: **the community is a trust engine for CTO retainers, not the primary revenue source.** The original strategy tried to make the community itself profitable and route overflow to an agency. The new strategy uses the community as a marketing channel that happens to generate some revenue.

Key improvements:
- Revenue comes from something you already do (CTO work) vs something that requires new infrastructure (agency projects)
- Price point is accessible ($250 vs $500) with a scarcity lever (20-member cap vs open enrollment)
- Moderator program is detailed and fair vs vague employee obligation
- Full financial model vs hand-wavy revenue targets
- Rejected alternatives are documented vs not considered

The main risk is the CTO conversion timeline. If CTO clients take 6 months instead of 3 to materialize, Months 3-8 will feel lean. The community itself won't generate meaningful owner income ($2,600/month net) until Month 6. Have a financial buffer for this gap.
